2  The Time Value of Money (TVM)

Welcome to the first module of Business Finance Foundations! In this module, we will explore one of the most fundamental concepts in finance: the Time Value of Money (TVM). Understanding TVM is crucial because it underpins virtually every financial decision you’ll make, from personal savings to corporate investment analysis.

2.1 The Core Intuition: Why Time Matters

If I offered you $1,000 today or $1,000 one year from today, which would you choose?

Almost everyone instinctively chooses the money today. But why? In finance, this isn’t just a preference; it’s a fundamental principle called the Time Value of Money (TVM).

2.1.1 The Three Pillars of TVM

1. Opportunity Cost: The “Earning Power” of Money

Formal Definition: Opportunity cost represents the return you forgo by not having money available to invest today.

In practice: If you have $1,000 today, you could invest it in a savings account, bonds, or stocks and earn a return. By waiting a year to receive that $1,000, you lose the opportunity to grow that money during the waiting period.

Example: If you could earn 5% in a savings account, having $1,000 today means you’d have $1,050 in one year. Waiting to receive $1,000 next year costs you that $50 in potential earnings.

Real-World Application: This is why lottery winners often choose lump-sum payments over annuities, and why companies evaluate projects based on their opportunity cost of capital.

2. Inflation: The “Purchasing Power” Erosion

Formal Definition: Inflation is the general increase in prices over time, which reduces the real purchasing power of money.

In practice: A dollar today buys more groceries, gas, or coffee than a dollar will next year because prices tend to rise over time.

Example: If inflation is 3% annually: - A $100 grocery basket today will cost $103 next year - Your $100 next year will only buy what $97.09 buys today - Over 10 years, that $100 will only have the purchasing power of about $74.41 in today’s terms

Historical Context: The U.S. has experienced average annual inflation of about 3% over the past century. During the 1970s, inflation reached double digits, dramatically illustrating how quickly purchasing power can erode.

3. Risk (Uncertainty): The “Will I Actually Get Paid?” Factor

Formal Definition: Risk represents the probability that promised future cash flows may not materialize as expected.

In practice: There’s always some chance that the person or company promising to pay you in the future might not be able to deliver. They could go bankrupt, default, or circumstances could change. Money in your hand today has zero “default risk.”

Example: Would you rather have $1,000 today from a friend, or a promise that your friend will pay you $1,000 in 5 years? Even if you trust your friend, a lot can happen in 5 years—job loss, relocation, financial hardship. The promise carries risk.

Financial Markets Application: This is why U.S. Treasury bonds (backed by the government) pay lower interest rates than corporate bonds—Treasuries have virtually no default risk, while corporations might fail.

2.1.2 Understanding the Discount Rate (Interest Rate)

The interest rate we use in TVM calculations isn’t arbitrary—it reflects these three components:

Discount Rate = Risk-Free Rate + Inflation Premium + Risk Premium

  • Risk-Free Rate: The “pure” time value of money—what you’d earn with zero risk (typically approximated by U.S. Treasury securities, currently around 2-4%)
  • Inflation Premium: Compensation for expected loss of purchasing power (historically ~2-3% in the U.S.)
  • Risk Premium: Extra return demanded for bearing uncertainty (varies widely: 1-2% for high-quality corporate bonds, 5-8% for stocks, 10%+ for venture capital)

Example: If you’re evaluating a corporate bond investment: - Risk-free rate: 3% (what Treasury bonds pay) - Expected inflation: 2% - Corporate risk premium: 2% - Total discount rate: 7%

This is why riskier investments require higher returns—investors demand compensation for bearing additional uncertainty.

Deep Dive: The Risk-Free Rate

In theory, the risk-free rate represents the return on an investment with zero default risk and zero reinvestment risk. In practice, we use short-term U.S. Treasury bills as a proxy because: - The U.S. government can print money to pay debts (no default risk) - Treasury bills mature in less than one year (minimal interest rate risk)

However, even Treasuries aren’t perfectly “risk-free”—they still carry inflation risk and the theoretical possibility of government default.

2.2 The “Magic” of Compounding

Compounding is the process where your money earns interest, and then that interest earns interest. This creates exponential growth rather than linear growth.

2.2.1 Simple Example

Imagine you invest $100 at a 10% annual interest rate:

  • Year 1: You earn $10 in interest. Your total is $110.
  • Year 2: You don’t just earn interest on your original $100; you earn 10% on the full $110. You earn $11 in interest. Your total is $121.
  • Year 3: You earn 10% on $121 = $12.10. Your total is $133.10.

Notice how the interest earned each year increases. That extra $1 in Year 2 and $2.10 in Year 3 is the result of compounding—earning interest on your interest.

2.2.2 The Power of Time

This “interest on interest” effect is what allows small savings to grow into large sums over long periods. Consider this dramatic example:

$10,000 invested at 8% annual interest: - After 10 years: $21,589 - After 20 years: $46,610 - After 30 years: $100,627 - After 40 years: $217,245

Notice that the growth accelerates over time. The fourth decade adds more than $116,000, while the first decade only adds about $11,500. This is the exponential nature of compounding.

Historical Example: Manhattan Island

The famous story of Manhattan being purchased for $24 in 1626 illustrates compounding’s power. If that $24 had been invested at 8% annual return, it would be worth over $200 trillion today—far more than Manhattan’s actual value. While this is a simplified example (finding 8% returns for 400 years is impossible), it demonstrates why starting early matters so much.

2.3 Future Value (FV) vs. Present Value (PV)

In this module, we focus on two primary directions of calculation:

  • Future Value (FV): Moving money forward in time. “If I save $X today, what will it be worth in the future?”
  • Present Value (PV): Moving money backward in time (often called Discounting). “If I need $X in the future, what is it worth to me today?”

2.3.1 The Basic Variables and Notation

To solve any TVM problem, we work with these four core variables. We’ll use consistent notation throughout this course:

  1. PV (Present Value): The current value of money; what a sum is worth today
  2. FV (Future Value): The value of money at a specific point in the future
  3. r (Interest Rate): The percentage rate of return per period (also called discount rate). In Excel, this parameter is called rate.
  4. n (Number of Periods): How long the money is invested or borrowed. In Excel, this parameter is called nper.

Key Principle: If you know any three of these variables, you can solve for the fourth. This is the foundation of all TVM calculations.

2.3.2 Visual Timeline

        PV                                    FV
         |------------------------------------>|
       Today    n periods at rate r        Future
    
    Compounding: Move PV forward to find FV
    Discounting: Move FV backward to find PV

2.4 Simple vs. Compound Interest

2.4.1 Simple Interest

Definition: Interest is calculated only on the original principal amount. Interest earned in previous periods does not earn additional interest.

Formula: \[\text{Simple Interest} = \text{Principal} \times \text{Rate} \times \text{Time}\]

Example: $1,000 at 10% simple interest for 5 years: - Year 1: Earn $100 (total: $1,100) - Year 2: Earn $100 (total: $1,200) - Year 3: Earn $100 (total: $1,300) - Year 4: Earn $100 (total: $1,400) - Year 5: Earn $100 (total: $1,500)

Total interest earned: $500

2.4.2 Compound Interest

Definition: Interest is calculated on the principal plus all accumulated interest from previous periods. This creates “interest on interest.”

Formula: \[FV = PV \times (1 + r)^n\]

Example: $1,000 at 10% compound interest for 5 years: - Year 1: Earn $100 on $1,000 (total: $1,100) - Year 2: Earn $110 on $1,100 (total: $1,210) - Year 3: Earn $121 on $1,210 (total: $1,331) - Year 4: Earn $133.10 on $1,331 (total: $1,464.10) - Year 5: Earn $146.41 on $1,464.10 (total: $1,610.51)

Total interest earned: $610.51

The Difference: Compound interest earned $110.51 more than simple interest over just 5 years. Over longer periods, this difference becomes enormous.

2.4.3 Which One is Used in Modern Finance?

Compound interest is the standard in virtually all modern financial applications, including: - Savings accounts and CDs - Bonds and loans - Stock market returns - Retirement accounts - Mortgage calculations

Simple interest is rarely used except in very short-term loans or specific legal contexts.

2.5 The Impact of Compounding Frequency

2.5.1 How Often Interest is Calculated Matters

Formal Definition: Compounding frequency refers to how many times per year interest is calculated and added to the principal.

In practice: The more frequently interest is calculated and added to your account, the more “interest on interest” you earn, even if the stated annual rate is the same.

2.5.2 Common Compounding Frequencies

Here’s how $1,000 grows at 6% annual interest for 1 year under different compounding frequencies:

Frequency Times per Year (m) Formula Future Value
Annual 1 \(1,000 × (1.06)^1\) $1,060.00
Semi-annual 2 \(1,000 × (1.03)^2\) $1,060.90
Quarterly 4 \(1,000 × (1.015)^4\) $1,061.36
Monthly 12 \(1,000 × (1.005)^12\) $1,061.68
Daily 365 \(1,000 × (1 + 0.06/365)^365\) $1,061.83
Continuous ∞ \(1,000 × e^{0.06}\) $1,061.84

Key Insight: Notice that going from annual to monthly compounding adds $1.68, but going from monthly to daily only adds $0.15. The benefit of more frequent compounding diminishes as frequency increases. There’s a mathematical limit (continuous compounding) beyond which you can’t go.

2.5.3 Excel Adjustment for Different Frequencies

When compounding is not annual, you must adjust both the rate and the number of periods:

Monthly compounding example: - Annual rate: 6% → Monthly rate: 6%/12 = 0.5% = 0.005 - Years: 5 → Months: 5 × 12 = 60

Excel formula:

=FV(0.06/12, 5*12, 0, -1000)

Result: $1,348.85 (compared to $1,338.23 with annual compounding)

2.5.4 APR vs. APY: What Banks Don’t Always Make Clear

APR (Annual Percentage Rate): The stated annual rate before accounting for compounding frequency. This is the “nominal” rate.

APY (Annual Percentage Yield): The effective annual rate after accounting for compounding frequency. This is what you actually earn.

Formula: \[APY = \left(1 + \frac{APR}{m}\right)^m - 1\]

Where m = number of compounding periods per year

Example: - 6% APR compounded monthly - APY = (1 + 0.06/12)^12 - 1 = (1.005)^12 - 1 = 0.06168 = 6.168%

Why This Matters: When comparing savings accounts or credit cards, always compare APY, not APR. A 5.9% APR compounded daily might be better than a 6.0% APR compounded annually.

APY Comparison Table:

APR Annual Quarterly Monthly Daily
5% 5.000% 5.095% 5.116% 5.127%
6% 6.000% 6.136% 6.168% 6.183%
7% 7.000% 7.186% 7.229% 7.250%

Consumer Protection Note: The Truth in Lending Act requires lenders to disclose APY precisely because APR alone can be misleading. When shopping for savings accounts, always ask for the APY. When comparing credit cards, the APR matters less than your payment behavior, but APY shows the true cost if you carry a balance.

2.6 The Mathematical Foundation

2.6.1 Future Value Formula

The fundamental formula for calculating future value with compound interest is:

\[FV = PV \times (1 + r)^n\]

Where: - \(FV\) = Future Value - \(PV\) = Present Value - \(r\) = Interest rate per period (as a decimal) - \(n\) = Number of periods

Example: What will $5,000 be worth in 10 years at 7% annual interest?

\[FV = 5,000 \times (1.07)^{10} = 5,000 \times 1.96715 = \$9,835.76\]

2.6.2 Present Value Formula

To find present value (discounting), we rearrange the future value formula:

\[PV = \frac{FV}{(1 + r)^n}\]

This can also be written as:

\[PV = FV \times (1 + r)^{-n}\]

Example: What is the present value of $10,000 to be received in 8 years if the discount rate is 6%?

\[PV = \frac{10,000}{(1.06)^8} = \frac{10,000}{1.59385} = \$6,274.12\]

Interpretation: You should be willing to pay $6,274.12 today for the right to receive $10,000 in 8 years, assuming a 6% discount rate.

2.6.3 The Relationship Between PV and FV

These formulas are inverses of each other: - Compounding (FV): Multiply by \((1 + r)^n\) - Discounting (PV): Divide by \((1 + r)^n\) (or multiply by \((1 + r)^{-n}\))

Key Insight: When interest rates are positive, PV is always less than FV. The higher the interest rate or the longer the time period, the greater the difference between PV and FV.

2.7 Financial Mental Models: The Rule of 72

2.7.1 Quick Estimation Tool

The Rule of 72 is a mental math shortcut used by financial professionals to estimate how long it takes for an investment to double at a given interest rate.

The Formula: \[\text{Years to Double} \approx \frac{72}{\text{Interest Rate (as whole number)}}\]

Examples: - At 6% interest: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years to double - At 8% interest: 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years to double - At 9% interest: 72 ÷ 9 = 8 years to double - At 12% interest: 72 ÷ 12 = 6 years to double

2.7.2 Verification

Let’s verify the 8% example: \[FV = PV \times (1.08)^9 = 1 \times 1.999 \approx 2.0\]

The Rule of 72 predicted 9 years, and the actual calculation shows 1.999—remarkably accurate!

2.7.3 Why It Works (The Math Behind It)

The Rule of 72 is a linear approximation of the logarithmic formula for doubling time:

\[n = \frac{\ln(2)}{\ln(1 + r)} \approx \frac{0.693}{r}\]

When r is expressed as a percentage, 0.693 × 100 ≈ 69.3, but 72 is used because it has more divisors (1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 9, 12, 18, 24, 36, 72), making mental math easier.

Accuracy: The Rule of 72 is remarkably accurate for interest rates between 6% and 10%. For rates outside this range, small errors accumulate, but it remains useful for quick estimates.

2.7.4 Reverse Application

You can also use the Rule of 72 in reverse: “If I want to double my money in 6 years, what return do I need?”

\[\text{Required Rate} = \frac{72}{\text{Years}} = \frac{72}{6} = 12\%\]

2.7.5 Related Rules

Deep Dive: Related Rules

  • Rule of 115: Estimates when money triples (115 ÷ rate)
    • At 8%: 115 ÷ 8 ≈ 14.4 years to triple
  • Rule of 144: Estimates when money quadruples (144 ÷ rate)
    • At 8%: 144 ÷ 8 = 18 years to quadruple

These are all linear approximations of exponential growth—handy for quick boardroom calculations when you don’t have a calculator.

2.7.6 Practical Applications

Retirement Planning: “I have $100,000 in my 401(k) at age 40. If it grows at 9% annually, how much will I have at 64?” - 24 years ÷ 8 years per doubling (72÷9) = 3 doublings - $100,000 → $200,000 → $400,000 → $800,000 - Quick estimate: ~$800,000 (actual: $806,862)

Inflation Impact: “If inflation is 3%, how long until my purchasing power is cut in half?” - 72 ÷ 3 = 24 years - A dollar today will have the purchasing power of 50 cents in 24 years

2.8 Moving to Excel

While formulas are important for understanding the logic, in the business world, we use Excel to handle calculations efficiently and accurately. Excel simplifies these calculations into built-in financial functions.

2.8.1 Core Excel TVM Functions

  • =FV(rate, nper, pmt, [pv], [type]) — Calculate future value
  • =PV(rate, nper, pmt, [fv], [type]) — Calculate present value
  • =RATE(nper, pmt, pv, [fv], [type]) — Calculate interest rate
  • =NPER(rate, pmt, pv, [fv], [type]) — Calculate number of periods

2.8.2 Important Excel Conventions and Defaults

1. Cash Flow Sign Convention

Excel uses a cash flow perspective where: - Negative numbers represent cash outflows (money leaving your pocket—investments, payments) - Positive numbers represent cash inflows (money coming to you—returns, receipts)

Example: If you invest $1,000 today at 5% for 10 years:

=FV(0.05, 10, 0, -1000)

The -1000 represents money leaving your pocket today. The result will be positive (approximately $1,629), representing money coming back to you in the future.

2. Interest Rates as Decimals

Always enter interest rates as decimals: - 5% = 0.05 - 7.5% = 0.075 - 12% = 0.12

3. Period Consistency

Ensure your interest rate period matches your time period: - Annual rate (6%) with annual periods (10 years) ✓ - Monthly rate (0.5%) with monthly periods (120 months) ✓ - Annual rate (6%) with monthly periods (120 months) ✗ WRONG

4. The [type] Parameter

The [type] parameter specifies when payments occur: - type = 0 (or omitted): End of period (default) - type = 1: Beginning of period (annuity due)

For single lump-sum problems in this module, omit [type]; Excel defaults to end-of-period (type = 0). In Module 2 when we cover annuities, we’ll explicitly use type = 1 for annuity due situations (like rent payments made at the beginning of each month).

2.8.3 Excel Function Examples

Example 1: Future Value You invest $2,000 today at 4% for 5 years. What will you have?

=FV(0.04, 5, 0, -2000)

Result: $2,433.31

Example 2: Present Value You need $25,000 in 10 years. How much must you invest today at 7%?

=PV(0.07, 10, 0, -25000)

Result: $12,715.64

Example 3: Interest Rate You invested $5,000 and it grew to $8,000 in 7 years. What was your annual return?

=RATE(7, 0, -5000, 8000)

Result: 0.0701 or 7.01%

Example 4: Number of Periods How long will it take $10,000 to grow to $20,000 at 6%?

=NPER(0.06, 0, -10000, 20000)

Result: 11.90 years

Verification with Rule of 72: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years (very close!)

2.9 Preview: Lump Sums vs. Annuities

In this module, we’ve focused exclusively on lump sum problems—single amounts of money at specific points in time.

Lump Sum Examples: - Investing $5,000 today in a savings account - Receiving a $10,000 inheritance in 3 years - A bond’s final principal payment at maturity - Winning a lottery prize paid as a single amount

2.9.1 What’s Coming in Module 2

In Module 2: Discounted Cash Flows, we’ll extend these concepts to annuities—regular, repeated payments over time.

Annuity Examples: - Monthly car payments ($350/month for 60 months) - Annual retirement account contributions ($6,000/year for 30 years) - Quarterly bond interest payments ($30/quarter for 10 years) - Mortgage payments ($1,500/month for 360 months)

The Connection: Annuities are just multiple lump sums strung together. Everything you’ve learned about PV and FV applies—we’ll just add the PMT (payment) parameter to our Excel functions.

Sneak Preview of Excel Functions for Annuities: - =PV(rate, nper, pmt, [fv]) — We’ll use the PMT parameter to find present value of payment streams - =PMT(rate, nper, pv, [fv]) — Solves for the payment amount (e.g., “What’s my monthly car payment?”) - =FV(rate, nper, pmt, [pv]) — Future value of regular payments (e.g., “How much will my retirement account be worth?”)

Don’t worry about mastering these now—just know that the TVM foundation you’re building in Module 1 applies to all future modules. Once you understand how to move a single dollar through time, moving multiple dollars through time is just an extension of the same logic.

2.10 Key Takeaways

By the end of this module, you should understand:

✓ The three pillars of TVM: Money today is worth more than money tomorrow due to opportunity cost, inflation, and risk

✓ Compounding creates exponential growth: “Interest on interest” is what allows small savings to grow into large sums over long periods

✓ FV moves forward; PV discounts backward: These are inverse operations along the time continuum

✓ The four core variables: PV, FV, r (rate), and n (periods)—know three, solve for the fourth

✓ Excel functions streamline TVM work: Master FV, PV, RATE, and NPER with proper sign conventions

✓ Compounding frequency matters: APY (effective rate) differs from APR (nominal rate) when compounding is more frequent than annual

✓ The Rule of 72: A powerful mental shortcut for estimating doubling time (Years ≈ 72 ÷ Rate)

✓ Discount rates reflect risk: Higher risk requires higher returns; the discount rate = risk-free rate + inflation premium + risk premium

✓ Time is your most valuable asset: Starting early with small amounts beats starting late with large amounts due to the exponential nature of compounding

2.11 Common Misconceptions and Pitfalls

2.11.1 Misconception 1: “Compound interest is only slightly better than simple interest”

Reality: Over long periods, the difference is enormous. For $10,000 at 8% over 30 years: - Simple interest: $34,000 - Compound interest: $100,627 - Difference: $66,627 (nearly 3x the original investment!)

2.11.2 Misconception 2: “I can wait a few years to start saving for retirement”

Reality: Due to compounding, delaying even 5 years can cost you hundreds of thousands of dollars. The Rule of 72 shows why: each doubling period you miss is exponentially costly.

2.11.3 Misconception 3: “A higher APR is always worse for borrowers”

Reality: You must compare APY, not APR. A 5.9% APR compounded daily might cost more than a 6.0% APR compounded annually.

2.11.4 Misconception 4: “Present value and future value are just different names for the same thing”

Reality: They move in opposite directions. PV discounts future money to today; FV compounds present money to the future. They’re mathematical inverses.

2.11.5 Misconception 5: “The Rule of 72 is just a rough guess”

Reality: For rates between 6-10%, the Rule of 72 is accurate to within a few months. It’s based on solid logarithmic mathematics, just simplified for mental calculation.

2.12 Connecting to Future Modules

The TVM concepts you’ve mastered in in this module are the foundation for everything that follows:

Discounted Cash Flows - Extends lump sums to annuities (regular payments) - Introduces perpetuities (payments that continue forever) - Applies TVM to loan amortization and retirement planning

Bond Valuation - Bonds are just a series of annuity payments (coupons) plus a lump sum (principal) - Bond prices move inversely with interest rates—now you understand why

Stock Valuation - Dividend discount models are PV calculations applied to uncertain future cash flows - The Gordon Growth Model is a perpetuity formula with growth

Risk and Return - Understanding how to measure investment risk using variance and standard deviation - Learning how the risk premium component of discount rates is determined - Applying the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) to estimate required returns based on systematic risk

Everything in finance is about moving cash flows through time. Master this, and you’ve mastered the language of finance.

- Corporate risk premium: 2%
- **Total discount rate: 7%**

This is why riskier investments require higher returns—investors demand compensation for bearing additional uncertainty.

> **Deep Dive: The Risk-Free Rate**
> 
> In theory, the risk-free rate represents the return on an investment with zero default risk and zero reinvestment risk. In practice, we use short-term U.S. Treasury bills as a proxy because:
> - The U.S. government can print money to pay debts (no default risk)
> - Treasury bills mature in less than one year (minimal interest rate risk)
> 
> However, even Treasuries aren't perfectly "risk-free"—they still carry inflation risk and the theoretical possibility of government default.

## The "Magic" of Compounding

Compounding is the process where your money earns interest, and then *that interest earns interest*. This creates exponential growth rather than linear growth.

### Simple Example

Imagine you invest $100 at a 10% annual interest rate:

*   **Year 1:** You earn $10 in interest. Your total is $110.
*   **Year 2:** You don't just earn interest on your original $100; you earn 10% on the full $110. You earn $11 in interest. Your total is $121.
*   **Year 3:** You earn 10% on $121 = $12.10. Your total is $133.10.

Notice how the interest earned each year increases. That extra $1 in Year 2 and $2.10 in Year 3 is the result of compounding—earning interest on your interest.

### The Power of Time

This "interest on interest" effect is what allows small savings to grow into large sums over long periods. Consider this dramatic example:

**$10,000 invested at 8% annual interest:**
- After 10 years: $21,589
- After 20 years: $46,610
- After 30 years: $100,627
- After 40 years: $217,245

Notice that the growth accelerates over time. The fourth decade adds more than $116,000, while the first decade only adds about $11,500. This is the exponential nature of compounding.

> **Historical Example: Manhattan Island**
> 
> The famous story of Manhattan being purchased for $24 in 1626 illustrates compounding's power. If that $24 had been invested at 8% annual return, it would be worth over $200 trillion today—far more than Manhattan's actual value. While this is a simplified example (finding 8% returns for 400 years is impossible), it demonstrates why starting early matters so much.

## Future Value (FV) vs. Present Value (PV)

In this module, we focus on two primary directions of calculation:

*   **Future Value (FV):** Moving money forward in time. "If I save $X today, what will it be worth in the future?"
*   **Present Value (PV):** Moving money backward in time (often called **Discounting**). "If I need $X in the future, what is it worth to me today?"

### The Basic Variables and Notation

To solve any TVM problem, we work with these four core variables. We'll use consistent notation throughout this course:

1.  **PV (Present Value):** The current value of money; what a sum is worth today
2.  **FV (Future Value):** The value of money at a specific point in the future
3.  **r (Interest Rate):** The percentage rate of return per period (also called discount rate). In Excel, this parameter is called `rate`.
4.  **n (Number of Periods):** How long the money is invested or borrowed. In Excel, this parameter is called `nper`.

**Key Principle:** If you know any three of these variables, you can solve for the fourth. This is the foundation of all TVM calculations.

### Visual Timeline

```
        PV                                    FV
         |------------------------------------>|
       Today    n periods at rate r        Future
    
    Compounding: Move PV forward to find FV
    Discounting: Move FV backward to find PV
```

## Simple vs. Compound Interest

### Simple Interest

**Definition:** Interest is calculated only on the original principal amount. Interest earned in previous periods does not earn additional interest.

**Formula:** 
$$\text{Simple Interest} = \text{Principal} \times \text{Rate} \times \text{Time}$$

**Example:** $1,000 at 10% simple interest for 5 years:
- Year 1: Earn $100 (total: $1,100)
- Year 2: Earn $100 (total: $1,200)
- Year 3: Earn $100 (total: $1,300)
- Year 4: Earn $100 (total: $1,400)
- Year 5: Earn $100 (total: $1,500)

Total interest earned: $500

---

### Compound Interest

**Definition:** Interest is calculated on the principal plus all accumulated interest from previous periods. This creates "interest on interest."

**Formula:**
$$FV = PV \times (1 + r)^n$$

**Example:** $1,000 at 10% compound interest for 5 years:
- Year 1: Earn $100 on $1,000 (total: $1,100)
- Year 2: Earn $110 on $1,100 (total: $1,210)
- Year 3: Earn $121 on $1,210 (total: $1,331)
- Year 4: Earn $133.10 on $1,331 (total: $1,464.10)
- Year 5: Earn $146.41 on $1,464.10 (total: $1,610.51)

Total interest earned: $610.51

**The Difference:** Compound interest earned $110.51 more than simple interest over just 5 years. Over longer periods, this difference becomes enormous.

### Which One is Used in Modern Finance?

**Compound interest** is the standard in virtually all modern financial applications, including:
- Savings accounts and CDs
- Bonds and loans
- Stock market returns
- Retirement accounts
- Mortgage calculations

Simple interest is rarely used except in very short-term loans or specific legal contexts.

## The Impact of Compounding Frequency

### How Often Interest is Calculated Matters

**Formal Definition:** Compounding frequency refers to how many times per year interest is calculated and added to the principal.

**In practice:** The more frequently interest is calculated and added to your account, the more "interest on interest" you earn, even if the stated annual rate is the same.

### Common Compounding Frequencies

Here's how $1,000 grows at 6% annual interest for 1 year under different compounding frequencies:

| Frequency | Times per Year (m) | Formula | Future Value |
|-----------|-------------------|---------|--------------|
| Annual | 1 | $1,000 × (1.06)^1$ | $1,060.00 |
| Semi-annual | 2 | $1,000 × (1.03)^2$ | $1,060.90 |
| Quarterly | 4 | $1,000 × (1.015)^4$ | $1,061.36 |
| Monthly | 12 | $1,000 × (1.005)^12$ | $1,061.68 |
| Daily | 365 | $1,000 × (1 + 0.06/365)^365$ | $1,061.83 |
| Continuous | ∞ | $1,000 × e^{0.06}$ | $1,061.84 |

**Key Insight:** Notice that going from annual to monthly compounding adds $1.68, but going from monthly to daily only adds $0.15. The benefit of more frequent compounding diminishes as frequency increases. There's a mathematical limit (continuous compounding) beyond which you can't go.

### Excel Adjustment for Different Frequencies

When compounding is not annual, you must adjust **both** the rate and the number of periods:

**Monthly compounding example:**
- Annual rate: 6% → Monthly rate: 6%/12 = 0.5% = 0.005
- Years: 5 → Months: 5 × 12 = 60

**Excel formula:**
```excel
=FV(0.06/12, 5*12, 0, -1000)
```

**Result:** $1,348.85 (compared to $1,338.23 with annual compounding)

### APR vs. APY: What Banks Don't Always Make Clear

**APR (Annual Percentage Rate):** The stated annual rate before accounting for compounding frequency. This is the "nominal" rate.

**APY (Annual Percentage Yield):** The effective annual rate after accounting for compounding frequency. This is what you actually earn.

**Formula:**
$$APY = \left(1 + \frac{APR}{m}\right)^m - 1$$

Where m = number of compounding periods per year

**Example:**
- 6% APR compounded monthly
- APY = (1 + 0.06/12)^12 - 1 = (1.005)^12 - 1 = 0.06168 = **6.168%**

**Why This Matters:** When comparing savings accounts or credit cards, always compare APY, not APR. A 5.9% APR compounded daily might be better than a 6.0% APR compounded annually.

**APY Comparison Table:**

| APR | Annual | Quarterly | Monthly | Daily |
|-----|--------|-----------|---------|-------|
| 5% | 5.000% | 5.095% | 5.116% | 5.127% |
| 6% | 6.000% | 6.136% | 6.168% | 6.183% |
| 7% | 7.000% | 7.186% | 7.229% | 7.250% |

> **Consumer Protection Note:** The Truth in Lending Act requires lenders to disclose APY precisely because APR alone can be misleading. When shopping for savings accounts, always ask for the APY. When comparing credit cards, the APR matters less than your payment behavior, but APY shows the true cost if you carry a balance.

## The Mathematical Foundation

### Future Value Formula

The fundamental formula for calculating future value with compound interest is:

$$FV = PV \times (1 + r)^n$$

Where:
- $FV$ = Future Value
- $PV$ = Present Value
- $r$ = Interest rate per period (as a decimal)
- $n$ = Number of periods

**Example:** What will $5,000 be worth in 10 years at 7% annual interest?

$$FV = 5,000 \times (1.07)^{10} = 5,000 \times 1.96715 = \$9,835.76$$

---

### Present Value Formula

To find present value (discounting), we rearrange the future value formula:

$$PV = \frac{FV}{(1 + r)^n}$$

This can also be written as:

$$PV = FV \times (1 + r)^{-n}$$

**Example:** What is the present value of $10,000 to be received in 8 years if the discount rate is 6%?

$$PV = \frac{10,000}{(1.06)^8} = \frac{10,000}{1.59385} = \$6,274.12$$

**Interpretation:** You should be willing to pay $6,274.12 today for the right to receive $10,000 in 8 years, assuming a 6% discount rate.

---

### The Relationship Between PV and FV

These formulas are inverses of each other:
- **Compounding** (FV): Multiply by $(1 + r)^n$
- **Discounting** (PV): Divide by $(1 + r)^n$ (or multiply by $(1 + r)^{-n}$)

**Key Insight:** When interest rates are positive, PV is always less than FV. The higher the interest rate or the longer the time period, the greater the difference between PV and FV.

## Financial Mental Models: The Rule of 72

### Quick Estimation Tool

The **Rule of 72** is a mental math shortcut used by financial professionals to estimate how long it takes for an investment to double at a given interest rate.

**The Formula:**
$$\text{Years to Double} \approx \frac{72}{\text{Interest Rate (as whole number)}}$$

**Examples:**
- At 6% interest: 72 ÷ 6 = **12 years** to double
- At 8% interest: 72 ÷ 8 = **9 years** to double
- At 9% interest: 72 ÷ 9 = **8 years** to double
- At 12% interest: 72 ÷ 12 = **6 years** to double

### Verification

Let's verify the 8% example:
$$FV = PV \times (1.08)^9 = 1 \times 1.999 \approx 2.0$$

The Rule of 72 predicted 9 years, and the actual calculation shows 1.999—remarkably accurate!

### Why It Works (The Math Behind It)

The Rule of 72 is a linear approximation of the logarithmic formula for doubling time:

$$n = \frac{\ln(2)}{\ln(1 + r)} \approx \frac{0.693}{r}$$

When r is expressed as a percentage, 0.693 × 100 ≈ 69.3, but **72 is used** because it has more divisors (1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 9, 12, 18, 24, 36, 72), making mental math easier.

**Accuracy:** The Rule of 72 is remarkably accurate for interest rates between 6% and 10%. For rates outside this range, small errors accumulate, but it remains useful for quick estimates.

### Reverse Application

You can also use the Rule of 72 in reverse: "If I want to double my money in 6 years, what return do I need?"

$$\text{Required Rate} = \frac{72}{\text{Years}} = \frac{72}{6} = 12\%$$

### Related Rules

> **Deep Dive: Related Rules**
> 
> - **Rule of 115:** Estimates when money **triples** (115 ÷ rate)
>   - At 8%: 115 ÷ 8 ≈ 14.4 years to triple
> - **Rule of 144:** Estimates when money **quadruples** (144 ÷ rate)
>   - At 8%: 144 ÷ 8 = 18 years to quadruple
> 
> These are all linear approximations of exponential growth—handy for quick boardroom calculations when you don't have a calculator.

### Practical Applications

**Retirement Planning:** "I have $100,000 in my 401(k) at age 40. If it grows at 9% annually, how much will I have at 64?"
- 24 years ÷ 8 years per doubling (72÷9) = 3 doublings
- $100,000 → $200,000 → $400,000 → $800,000
- Quick estimate: ~$800,000 (actual: $806,862)

**Inflation Impact:** "If inflation is 3%, how long until my purchasing power is cut in half?"
- 72 ÷ 3 = 24 years
- A dollar today will have the purchasing power of 50 cents in 24 years

## Moving to Excel

While formulas are important for understanding the logic, in the business world, we use Excel to handle calculations efficiently and accurately. Excel simplifies these calculations into built-in financial functions.

### Core Excel TVM Functions

*   `=FV(rate, nper, pmt, [pv], [type])` — Calculate future value
*   `=PV(rate, nper, pmt, [fv], [type])` — Calculate present value
*   `=RATE(nper, pmt, pv, [fv], [type])` — Calculate interest rate
*   `=NPER(rate, pmt, pv, [fv], [type])` — Calculate number of periods

### Important Excel Conventions and Defaults

**1. Cash Flow Sign Convention**

Excel uses a **cash flow perspective** where:
- **Negative numbers** represent cash **outflows** (money leaving your pocket—investments, payments)
- **Positive numbers** represent cash **inflows** (money coming to you—returns, receipts)

**Example:** If you invest $1,000 today at 5% for 10 years:
```excel
=FV(0.05, 10, 0, -1000)
```
The -1000 represents money leaving your pocket today. The result will be positive (approximately $1,629), representing money coming back to you in the future.

**2. Interest Rates as Decimals**

Always enter interest rates as decimals:
- 5% = 0.05
- 7.5% = 0.075
- 12% = 0.12

**3. Period Consistency**

Ensure your interest rate period matches your time period:
- Annual rate (6%) with annual periods (10 years) ✓
- Monthly rate (0.5%) with monthly periods (120 months) ✓
- Annual rate (6%) with monthly periods (120 months) ✗ **WRONG**

**4. The [type] Parameter**

The `[type]` parameter specifies when payments occur:
- **type = 0** (or omitted): End of period (default)
- **type = 1**: Beginning of period (annuity due)

**For single lump-sum problems in this module, omit [type]**; Excel defaults to end-of-period (type = 0). In Module 2 when we cover annuities, we'll explicitly use type = 1 for annuity due situations (like rent payments made at the beginning of each month).

### Excel Function Examples

**Example 1: Future Value**
You invest $2,000 today at 4% for 5 years. What will you have?

```excel
=FV(0.04, 5, 0, -2000)
```
**Result:** $2,433.31

---

**Example 2: Present Value**
You need $25,000 in 10 years. How much must you invest today at 7%?

```excel
=PV(0.07, 10, 0, -25000)
```
**Result:** $12,715.64

---

**Example 3: Interest Rate**
You invested $5,000 and it grew to $8,000 in 7 years. What was your annual return?

```excel
=RATE(7, 0, -5000, 8000)
```
**Result:** 0.0701 or 7.01%

---

**Example 4: Number of Periods**
How long will it take $10,000 to grow to $20,000 at 6%?

```excel
=NPER(0.06, 0, -10000, 20000)
```
**Result:** 11.90 years

**Verification with Rule of 72:** 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years (very close!)

## Common Misconceptions and Pitfalls

### Misconception 1: "Compound interest is only slightly better than simple interest"

**Reality:** Over long periods, the difference is enormous. For $10,000 at 8% over 30 years:
- Simple interest: $34,000
- Compound interest: $100,627
- **Difference: $66,627** (nearly 3x the original investment!)

### Misconception 2: "I can wait a few years to start saving for retirement"

**Reality:** Due to compounding, delaying even 5 years can cost you hundreds of thousands of dollars. The Rule of 72 shows why: each doubling period you miss is exponentially costly.

### Misconception 3: "A higher APR is always worse for borrowers"

**Reality:** You must compare APY, not APR. A 5.9% APR compounded daily might cost more than a 6.0% APR compounded annually.

### Misconception 4: "Present value and future value are just different names for the same thing"

**Reality:** They move in opposite directions. PV discounts future money to today; FV compounds present money to the future. They're mathematical inverses.

### Misconception 5: "The Rule of 72 is just a rough guess"

**Reality:** For rates between 6-10%, the Rule of 72 is accurate to within a few months. It's based on solid logarithmic mathematics, just simplified for mental calculation.
